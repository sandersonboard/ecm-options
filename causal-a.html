✦ Root cause attribution

The Q3 finding is a detection by IT.AC.1.C01, not a failure of IT.AC.1.C01. The control performed as designed — it caught the unauthorized escalation. The actual gap is in 5.1.2 (change approval): the emergency-access path doesn't require a post-event approval ticket, so an escalation can happen without leaving a trail until the quarterly review. Fixing 4.2.1 won't prevent recurrence; fixing 5.1.2 will.

The strategic claim. Most GRC tools file a finding and stop. Causal attribution turns one finding into two outcomes: fix what surfaced and fix what caused. Over a year, that's the difference between “same Q3 finding every Q3” and a control catalog that learns. The audit committee notices, the external auditor notices, the deficiency rate drops.

State 3. Two tickets created from one finding. The escalation gets rolled back in 24 hours (symptom). The change-approval policy gets rewritten in Q4 (cause). Next year's Q3 review doesn't see this finding again — not because the access-review control got better, but because the upstream control stopped letting the gap exist. Causal trace inconclusive · finding stays at IT.AC.1.C01

A different finding (anomalous data export, 2026-09-22 22:08 UTC) traces back into the connector graph but the walk stops before reaching a root cause ECM is confident about.

What ECM tried

Started from the symptom (a large data export from prod-db-01 by an authorized account, at a time when no scheduled job was running). Walked backward through connectors:

  • Okta — account used was active, no escalation in the prior 30 days. ✓ Clean.
  • ServiceNow — no incident or CR ticket associated with the export.
  • Workday — account holder still employed, no role change recently.
  • GitHub — no automation/script change pushed in the 7 days before the export.
  • Databricks/Snowflake — no scheduled query ran. Manual ad-hoc query, no recorded business purpose.

Every upstream surface checks out individually. There's no single control whose design weakness explains this. The most likely cause is off-policy ad-hoc behavior by an authorized actor — which is exactly the kind of thing automated trace doesn't resolve.

⚠ ECM is honest about its limits

Confidence on root-cause attribution: 42% — below the 70% threshold ECM uses to propose remediation. Rather than guess at a control to fix (which would route to an owner who can't actually solve the problem), ECM hands the trace to Marie for human investigation, with the partial walk attached.
